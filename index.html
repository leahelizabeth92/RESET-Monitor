What each chart counts

Drug offenses
Every SFPD incident whose subcategory is “Drug Violation” — possession, possession for sale, sale, and transport of controlled substances, plus public drug intoxication. These appear under three parent categories in the raw data (“Drug Offense,” “Drug Violation,” and “Disorderly Conduct”); this chart combines them all so every drug violation is counted here exactly once.
“Suspicious person” calls & stops
Law-enforcement dispatched records whose final call type is “Suspicious Person,” split by who initiated them: 911 calls from the public (records where someone called it in) versus officer-initiated “on-view” records, where an officer or city street team observed something on patrol and generated the record themselves (includes HSOC street-team referrals). The two measure different things — community reporting versus police activity. Excludes records cancelled by dispatch (disposition CAN), cleared as no merit (22), or marked duplicate (DUP).
Larceny, violent crime & burglary
SFPD incident reports, stacked in three groups: larceny theft (the “Larceny Theft” category — thefts without force or breaking in, including from vehicles and shoplifting), violent crime (the FBI definition: homicide, rape, robbery, and aggravated assault — simple assault is not included), and burglary (the “Burglary” category — unlawful entry of a building). Motor vehicle theft is not included in any group.
Homelessness-related 311 requests
311 cases of type “General Request” routed to the Department of Homelessness & Supportive Housing. No prior-year line on this chart: the city only began routing cases this way in mid-2025, so a year-over-year comparison would reflect the process change, not conditions on the street.
How officer-initiated “suspicious person” stops end
Each stop’s outcome comes from the disposition code police record when closing the dispatch record. Column by column:
  • Stops/mo — the average number of officer-initiated “suspicious person” stops per month during the period.
  • Handled / advised — the stop ended on the scene with no formal action: the officer resolved the situation, gave a warning, or told the person to move along (codes HAN, ADV). No paperwork results.
  • Arrest — the person was taken into custody (code ARR). The charge is not necessarily drug-related — warrants and other offenses are common.
  • Citation — the person was issued a ticket/citation rather than arrested (code CIT).
  • Report — the officer wrote a formal incident report, documenting something without an arrest or citation (code REP).
  • Other — everything else: no disposition recorded, determined to have no merit, person gone before contact, unable to locate, etc.
Percentages are each outcome’s share of that neighborhood’s stops in the period. Public 911 calls are not included in this table — only stops officers initiated themselves.
Overdose-related 911 responses
Opioid overdose-related 911 calls responded to by ambulances, per SF Emergency Medical Services patient-care records. Citywide only.
Drug overdose deaths (preliminary)
Preliminary count of unintentional fatal drug overdoses from the Office of the Chief Medical Examiner’s monthly report. Counts are revised as investigations conclude. Citywide only.

“Show as % of citywide” re-draws each neighborhood chart as that neighborhood’s share of the citywide total for the same period (e.g., SoMa’s drug offenses as a percentage of all SF drug offenses) — a scale-free way to see whether activity is concentrating in or leaving a neighborhood. In this mode the prior-year line and comparison dots become shares too, and stacked charts show the neighborhood’s total share. The two citywide overdose charts are unaffected.

The circle and diamond dots on each neighborhood chart show the other two neighborhoods’ totals for the same period, for side-by-side comparison (also listed in each chart’s tooltip and table). The gray line on each chart shows the same period one year earlier (weekly views compare the same-weekday week 52 weeks back; in-progress periods compare only the days elapsed so far). On the suspicious-person chart the solid gray line is last year’s total and the dashed amber line is last year’s public 911 calls only, so each stacked layer has its own comparison. The dashed vertical line marks the RESET Center opening on May 4, 2026.

The city’s Police Incident Code Crosswalk lists every raw incident code and the category/subcategory it rolls into; the SFPD dataset explainer and 311 dataset explainer describe how each dataset is produced.

Sources (SF Open Data portal): Police Incident Reports · Dispatched Calls for Service · 311 Cases · Overdose-Related 911 Responses (EMS) · Preliminary Unintentional Drug Overdose Deaths

The neighborhood tabs use the city’s official Analysis Neighborhood boundaries (“South of Market,” “Tenderloin,” “Mission”), the standard geography stamped on DataSF datasets. Overdose 911 responses and overdose deaths are published citywide only — DPH releases neighborhood-level overdose data solely in quarterly PDF reports, so those two charts do not change with the selected tab. Police and 311 counts reflect reports/dispatches, not verified incidents, and recent days may still be loading into the city portal. Death counts are preliminary medical-examiner determinations and are revised. EMS overdose data is published on a ~3-month lag.
